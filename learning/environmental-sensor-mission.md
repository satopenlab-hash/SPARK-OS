## Mission title

Environmental Sensor Mission: Proposing a Classroom Improvement

## Driving question

How can we use simple environmental data from our own classroom to propose one evidence-based improvement to our learning space?

## Learner goal

Collect a small set of real environmental sensor observations (temperature, light level, and noise level) over the course of one school day, organize the data into a simple chart, identify one specific pattern, and propose one concrete, evidence-based improvement to the classroom or school environment.

## Real-world context

Schools regularly make small facilities decisions — adjusting lighting schedules, seating layout, or quiet hours — without collecting any data first. Low-cost sensors make it possible for learners to gather real evidence about their own environment and practice connecting a specific observation to a specific, feasible recommendation. This mirrors how facilities and sustainability teams actually work, at a scale appropriate for one classroom and one day.

## Expected artifact

- A one-page "data story" combining a simple chart and a short written explanation.
- A chart or table showing sensor readings across the school day.
- A written proposal (2–4 sentences) recommending one specific improvement, tied directly to the data.
- A completed reflection.

## Evidence learners will collect

- Timestamped environmental sensor readings (temperature, light level, noise level).
- A short written explanation identifying one pattern in the readings.
- A proposed improvement with a stated rationale.
- Reflection notes, including at least one change made after feedback.

## AI use plan

AI may help with:
- Brainstorming which environmental factors might be worth measuring, and why.
- Helping organize raw readings into a simple chart or table.
- Asking clarifying or reflection questions about the pattern the learner identified.
- Explaining vocabulary (e.g., "baseline," "correlation," "sensor drift") at an age-appropriate level.
- Drafting a rough first-pass chart caption for the learner to revise in their own words.

AI may not:
- Invent, estimate, or "fill in" any sensor reading the learner did not actually collect.
- Write the learner's final data story or reflection on their behalf.
- Decide which improvement to propose.
- Issue a grade, score, or badge.
- Access or reference any other learner's private data.

## Teacher role

The teacher approves this mission before use, supervises data collection (real but non-identifying environmental readings only — never paired with student names, photos, or seating charts), checks that the learner's claim actually matches their evidence, confirms the learner can explain their own reasoning independent of any AI assistance used, and signs off before the artifact is considered for any recognition record.

## Learner reflection prompts

1. What was your goal for this mission?
2. What did you collect or test?
3. What evidence supports your proposed improvement?
4. Where did AI help, if you used AI?
5. What did you decide yourself, independent of AI suggestions?
6. What changed after feedback from your teacher or a peer?
7. What would you try next?

## Rubric dimensions

| Dimension | Evidence expected | Human reviewer |
|---|---|---|
| Data collection | At least 3 timestamped readings per measured variable, recorded honestly, including anomalies | Teacher |
| Pattern identification | Learner names one specific pattern and points to the specific readings that support it | Teacher |
| Proposed improvement | Improvement is concrete, feasible, and tied directly to the identified pattern | Teacher |
| Reflection quality | Learner can explain, in their own words, where AI helped and where they made the call themselves | Teacher |
| Revision | Evidence of at least one change made after feedback | Teacher |

## Privacy check

- [ ] No personally identifiable student data in public records.
- [ ] Any photos or media have appropriate authorization.
- [ ] Public examples are synthetic or de-identified.

*Note: environmental readings (temperature, light, noise) are not student PII by themselves. They only become sensitive if paired with a name, photo, or seating assignment — the teacher confirms this checklist at the time of actual use, not at template-fill time.*

## Stop condition

A teacher has reviewed the learner's data story and proposed improvement, and the learner has completed and can explain their reflection, including one revision made after feedback.

## Portfolio destination

`recognition/portfolios/` (private, per-learner). Only a synthetic demonstration version of this mission — not real learner output — belongs in the public repository, e.g. `examples/classroom-mission/`.

## Recognition eligibility

A teacher-reviewed data story, a specific proposed improvement tied to evidence, and a completed reflection showing at least one revision. On its own, this mission supports at most **Evidence Level 3** (Artifact + learner reflection) per `skills/recognition.md`. Reaching Level 4 requires the additional teacher validation record described there.
